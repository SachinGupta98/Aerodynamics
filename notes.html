🌬️ 1. Aerodynamics

1.1 Introduction & Definition

Aerodynamics is a sub-field of fluid dynamics and gas dynamics. The term is often used synonymously with gas dynamics — the difference being that gas dynamics applies to all gases, not just air.

  • Aerodynamics is primarily concerned with the forces of Drag and Lift.
  • Engineers apply aerodynamic principles to aircraft, automobiles, buildings, bridges, and even soccer balls.
  • The science of building and operating an aircraft is called Aeronautics.
  • Aerodynamics explains how an airplane is able to fly.
  • Anything that moves through air is affected by aerodynamics — from rockets to kites to cars.
ADAG – Aerodynamics is About Drag And lift (plus thrust & Gravity/weight). Think of a plane: it needs more lift than weight and more thrust than drag.
1.2 The Four Forces of Flight
ForceDirectionOpposesDescription
LiftUpward ↑WeightGenerated by wings; wings are curved on top, flatter on bottom → lower pressure on top → upward push
WeightDownward ↓LiftForce of gravity × mass of object
ThrustForward →DragForce pushing the aircraft forward (propeller, jet engine)
DragBackward ←ThrustResistance force; round & narrow surfaces = less drag
LIFT ↑ │ DRAG ←──── ✈️ AIRCRAFT ────→ THRUST │ WEIGHT ↓ Fly condition: LIFT > WEIGHT && THRUST > DRAG

How Wings Provide Lift (Bernoulli's Principle)

  • Wings are curved on top, flatter on bottom (aerofoil shape).
  • Air flows faster over the curved top → lower pressure on top.
  • Higher pressure below pushes the wing upward → LIFT.
  • Same principle applies to helicopter rotor blades, kites, and sailboat sails.
Lift ka funda simple hai: Curved upar wali surface pe hawa zyada fast chalti hai, pressure kam hota hai. Neeche se zyada pressure push karta hai upar — bas airplane ud jaata hai! Yahi kaam helicopter, kite aur sailboat mein bhi hota hai.
  • For flight: Lift > Weight and Thrust > Drag.
  • Curved wing shape → differential air speed → pressure difference → lift.
  • Round/narrow surfaces have less drag than flat/wide ones.
  • A glider has no thrust — it slows and lands as drag acts on it.

⚡ 2. Electric Vehicles (EVs)

2.1 Introduction & History

Electric vehicles (EVs) represent a significant shift from traditional internal combustion engines (ICEs) toward electric propulsion systems. EVs use electricity stored in rechargeable batteries to power electric motors.

Historical Context

  • EVs predate gasoline-powered vehicles (early 19th century).
  • Limitations in battery technology & cheap fossil fuels led to ICE dominance throughout the 20th century.
  • 21st-century resurgence driven by:

2.5 Tractive Effort
Tractive Effort = Force generated by the EV propulsion system to overcome resistance and accelerate the vehicle. More specifically, it is the force transmitted between the drive wheels and the road surface.
ConceptDetails
Definition & CalculationFtractive = Motor torque × Gear ratio / Wheel radius
Tractive Effort CurveHigh TE at low speed (constant torque), decreasing at high speed (constant power)
Resistance ForcesRolling resistance, aerodynamic drag, gradient resistance
Net Tractive EffortTE minus all resistance forces = acceleration force
Performance ParametersMax speed, gradeability, acceleration
Limiting FactorsTyre–road friction, motor peak torque, battery power limit
2.6 Transmission Requirements

Unlike ICE vehicles, EVs have simpler transmission requirements because electric motors deliver high torque across a wide speed range.

Transmission Configurations

  • Single-Speed Transmission – Most common; one fixed gear ratio
  • Multi-Speed Transmission – Improves efficiency & performance across wider speed range
  • Direct Drive – Motor directly coupled to wheels; no gearbox

Transmission Design Considerations

  • Gear Ratio Selection – Balances max speed vs. acceleration
  • NVH (Noise, Vibration, Harshness) – EVs are quieter, so transmission noise becomes noticeable
  • Efficiency – Mechanical power transfer efficiency 95–98%
  • Design Integration – Motor + gearbox as a single unit
  • Control Integration – Electronic management of torque delivery

🚀 3. Jet Propulsion

3.1 What is Jet Propulsion?
Jet Propulsion: Propulsion of a body by the discharge of fluid. A jet engine propels bodies such as rockets, ships, and aircraft.
  • Based on Newton's 2nd and 3rd Laws of Motion.
  • A jet propulsion engine is a reaction engine — it discharges a fast-moving fluid jet to generate thrust in the opposite direction.
  • Thrust = the force that moves the body forward using propulsive devices.
  • In a jet propulsion engine, the gas turbine drives the compressor and auxiliary equipment — not directly produces propulsive power.
  • Propeller: smaller acceleration to a large mass of air.
  • Jet engine: larger acceleration to a smaller mass of air.
Newton's 3rd Law: "For every action (hot gas out the back), there is an equal & opposite reaction (plane goes forward)." 🎈 Like releasing a balloon — air shoots out, balloon flies!

3.3 I.C. Engine Driven Propulsive System
  • Air-breathing engine — uses air as working fluid.
  • Net power drives a propeller to create thrust.
  • Uses air-cooled, multi-cylinder, radial I.C. engine.
  • Speed limit: 700 km/h.
  • Used for short-range flights and widely used in helicopters.
3.4 Ramjet Engine
Ramjet is a steady combustion / continuous flow air-breathing engine. It is the simplest of all air-breathing engines.

Construction

  • Consists of: supersonic diffuser → subsonic diffuser → combustion chamber (with fuel injector) → discharge nozzle.
  • No moving parts.
  • Combustion occurs at subsonic speed of airflow.
  • Used in guided-missile systems (defense sector).
AdvantagesDisadvantages
Simple construction — no moving parts (no compressor/turbine)Very high fuel consumption at low/moderate speeds
Very cheap, almost no maintenanceCannot start on its own — needs external launch device
Greater thrust/weight than any propulsion engine at supersonic speed (except rocket)Requires flame holder to stabilize combustion
Low specific fuel consumption at high speed & altitude vs. turbojet—
Can use any liquid fuel or solid/nuclear fuels—
3.5 Pulsejet Engine
A Pulsejet is an engine that works on combustion in pulses (phases). Usually built with no moving parts.

Cycle

  1. Air flows through intake (1) and mixes with fuel (2).
  2. Valve (3) closes; ignited fuel-air mix (4) propels exhaust.
AdvantagesDisadvantages
Simple construction, no moving parts — light, carries high payloadVery high noise and severe vibrations (intermittent pulses)
Sufficient thrust at low speedsHigh fuel consumption, very low thermal efficiency
Suitable for bombers and target missiles (unmanned)Useful only 30–60 minutes flight life
—Affected by atmospheric air density → limited altitude
3.6 Turbojet Engine (Jet Propulsion context)
  • Air-breathing and gas turbine propulsive system.
  • Thrust produced by acceleration of hot combustion gases through the exhaust nozzle.
  • A reaction engine.
  • Thrust can be substantially increased by using an afterburner.
AdvantagesDisadvantages
Simple construction vs. I.C. engine for same powerVery poor fuel economy at low flight speeds
Weight-power ratio = ¼ that of I.C. engine systemInefficient below 550 km/h

3.7 Turboprop Engine
A Turboprop = jet engine attached to a propeller. Intermediate between turbojet and I.C. engine + propeller. Also called propjet.
  • Uses increased mass flow of air for better fuel economy.
  • Modern turboprops use smaller-diameter, more-bladed propellers for efficiency at higher speeds.
AdvantagesDisadvantages
Operates at any speed up to 800 km/hPropulsive efficiency drops drastically at high speed
High thrust at low speed → short runway takeoffSuitable only for short range, low altitude, low speed
Thrust reversal easy by varying blade angleRequires reduction gear → added cost & weight
3.8 Turbofan Engine

The bypass system increases thrust without increasing fuel consumption by:

  • Increasing total air-mass flow.
  • Reducing exhaust velocity.
  • Using the same total energy supply.

Most modern commercial jet airliners use high-bypass turbofan engines for fuel efficiency.

3.9 Rocket Engines (Non-Air Breathing)
Rocket engines carry their own oxidizer — they do not need atmospheric air. Hence they can operate in vacuum (space).

Types

TypeFuel & OxidizerComplexityNote
Solid PropellantPre-mixed solid fuel+oxidizerSimple (no valves/pumps)Earliest rockets (gunpowder). Case = combustion chamber.
Liquid PropellantAlcohol (fuel) + Liquid O₂ (oxidizer)Complex (pumps, valves, sensors)High performance; controllable thrust

Rocket Thrust Formula

Thrust = Momentum Thrust + Pressure Thrust T = (ṁ_ox + ṁ_f) × V_e + (P_e - P_a) × A_e Where: ṁ_ox = mass flow rate of oxidizer (kg/s) ṁ_f = mass flow rate of fuel (kg/s) V_e = effective exit/jet velocity (m/s) P_e = exhaust jet pressure (Pa) P_a = atmospheric pressure (Pa) A_e = exit jet area (m²) For a rocket (oxidizer carried onboard): Entry velocity of oxidizer = 0 T = ṁ_prop × C (where C = effective jet velocity)

🛩️ 4. Turbojet Engines (Detailed Study)

4.1 Introduction
  • Turbojets are the oldest kind of general-purpose jet engines.
  • They are rotary engines that extract energy from combustion gas flow.
  • Produce thrust by increasing the velocity of air flowing through the engine.
  • Operate on Newton's 3rd Law: "For every action there is an equal and opposite reaction."

4.3 Working of a Turbojet Engine
  1. Air intake: Air drawn into rotating compressor via intake.
  2. Compression: Air compressed to higher pressure (also raises temperature).
  3. Combustion: Fuel mixed with compressed air; ignited by flame in eddy of flame holder. Temperature rises dramatically.
  4. Expansion through Turbine: Hot combustion gases expand through turbine. ~2/3 of power drives the compressor.
  5. Exhaust Nozzle: Remaining gas expands to ambient pressure, producing high-velocity jet → thrust.
ACTEN – Air intake → Compress → burn (combusT) → Expand through turbine → Nozzle (exhaust).
4.4 Afterburner (Reheat)
An afterburner (or "reheat jet-pipe") is added to the rear of the jet engine. It sprays fuel directly into the hot exhaust where it ignites, significantly boosting thrust.
  • Drawback: very high fuel consumption rate.
  • Used by military fighter jets for short bursts of extra thrust.
4.5 Thrust Reversers
A thrust reverser is a pair of clamshell doors at the rear of the engine. When deployed, they divert thrust to help slow an aircraft upon landing.
  • Accidental deployment during flight is extremely dangerous — can cause loss of control and aircraft destruction.
4.6 Materials Used in Turbojet Engines

Operating Challenges

  • Thousands of operating hours at temperatures up to 1,100°C (2,000°F)
  • High thermal stresses (rapid temperature changes & gradients)
  • High mechanical stresses (high RPM, aerodynamic forces)
  • Low- and high-frequency vibration loading
  • Must resist: Oxidation, Corrosion, Creep, Fatigue (HCF & LCF), Stress rupture
